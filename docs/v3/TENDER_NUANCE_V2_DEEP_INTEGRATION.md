## 1B. The Translation Rules (For Developers)

1. **Never use the word "sacred"** in user-facing text. Use "alive," "living," "present," or "the space between."
2. **Never use "consciousness"** in user-facing text. Use "awareness," "presence," or "what you notice."
3. **"Pattern" is the bridge word** — it lives naturally in both Nuance and clinical language. Use it freely.
4. **"Field" is acceptable** but always paired with concrete language: "the field between you" or "the relational field" — never just "the field."
5. **"Emerge/emergence" are acceptable** — they're clinical (EFT uses them) AND philosophical.
6. **The body is always the anchor.** When Nuance goes abstract, the app goes somatic: "Where do you feel this?"

# PART 2: ASSESSMENT INTEGRATION — COMPREHENSIVE

## 2A. NEW INSTRUMENT: "The Space Between Us" — Relational Field Awareness Scale

*20 items. 7-point Likert (1 = Strongly Disagree to 7 = Strongly Agree).*  
*Administered individually. Scores compared across partners.*  
*Measures: Awareness of, investment in, and capacity for the relational field.*

### Subscale 1: Field Recognition (5 items)
*Source: Nuance's "The Sacred Between" / "WEARE" — Do you sense the "we"?*
*Clinical grounding: Couple identity research, PACT couple bubble, co-regulation (Polyvagal)*

1. When my partner and I are truly connected, something emerges between us that feels larger than either of us alone.
2. I can sense when the space between us shifts — when it feels warm or when it feels cold.
3. Our relationship has a quality or atmosphere that changes depending on how present we both are.
4. I sometimes notice that my partner and I create something together (a mood, an energy, a feeling) that neither of us could create on our own.
5. There are moments when I feel the relationship itself is communicating what it needs.

### Subscale 2: Holding Creative Tension (5 items)
*Source: Nuance's "Tension Truth" / "Creative Tension" — Can you stay with difference?*
*Clinical grounding: Differentiation of Self (Bowen), perpetual problems (Gottman), dialectical thinking (DBT)*

6. When my partner and I disagree, I can hold both perspectives without needing to resolve the tension immediately.
7. I believe our differences can generate something new rather than just creating conflict.
8. I can tolerate not knowing whether my partner is "right" or "wrong" during a disagreement.
9. I find that sitting with unresolved tension between us sometimes leads to unexpected understanding.
10. I see our personality differences as resources for the relationship, not just sources of friction.

### Subscale 3: Presence and Attunement (5 items)
*Source: Nuance's "Presence" principle / "What is here?" — Can you show up?*
*Clinical grounding: Mindfulness in relationships (ACT), attunement (EFT), bid recognition (Gottman)*

11. I regularly pause to notice what's happening between my partner and me before reacting.
12. When I'm with my partner, I'm usually fully present rather than preoccupied with other things.
13. I can sense when my partner needs something from me even before they say it.
14. I find it easy to slow down and really listen when my partner is speaking, even if I disagree.
15. I notice patterns in how we interact — certain rhythms or cycles that repeat.

### Subscale 4: Emergent Orientation (5 items)
*Source: Nuance's "Eternal Beginning" / "Field Knowing" — Do you trust what emerges?*
*Clinical grounding: ACT willingness/acceptance, EFT emotional engagement, PACT implicit relational knowing*

16. I believe that when both my partner and I are truly present, wisdom about our relationship naturally emerges.
17. I trust that our relationship can heal itself if we both show up honestly.
18. I am willing to let go of my plan for how a conversation should go and follow what actually unfolds.
19. I believe the relationship knows things that neither of us individually knows.
20. I am open to being changed by what emerges between us.

### Scoring

**Total (20-140):**
- 20-60: Low field awareness — patterns run you; you're inside the cycle, not observing it
- 61-100: Moderate — you sense the field in calm moments but lose it under stress
- 101-140: High — strong capacity to orient toward the "we" as a living entity

**Subscale Scores (5-35 each):**
Each subscale produces a T-score (mean=25, possible range 5-35).

## 2B. NEW INSTRUMENT: "What Lives Between You" — Couple Field Assessment

*15 items. Administered AFTER both partners complete individual assessments.*  
*Each partner answers independently. Results are compared.*  
*This is the first "couple-level" data point in the app.*

### Section 1: Our Pattern (5 items)
*Source: "Pattern as Primary Reality" — translated through EFT cycle mapping*

1. Describe in one sentence what happens between you when things go wrong. *(Free text)*
2. When this pattern starts, I usually: *(a) move toward my partner, (b) move away, (c) freeze, (d) fight*
3. When this pattern starts, I believe my partner is: *(a) pulling away, (b) attacking, (c) not caring, (d) trying to control*
4. "I can see this pattern without blaming either of us." *(1-7 Likert)*
5. "I believe this pattern is trying to protect us both, even though it hurts." *(1-7 Likert)*

**Item 5 is the philosophical integration point.** Clinically, this is EFT cycle de-escalation. Philosophically, this is Nuance's recognition that the pattern itself has intelligence — it emerged to protect, even as it now constrains.

**Pattern Comparison Algorithm:**
- Compare Item 2 across partners → Detect pursue-withdraw, mutual pursuit, mutual withdrawal, or asymmetric pattern
- Compare Item 3 across partners → Detect attribution gap (what I think you're doing vs. what you think you're doing)
- Compare Item 1 free text → NLP similarity analysis: are they describing the same cycle?
- Item 4 gap score → How different are their cycle-awareness levels?

### Section 2: Our Resources (5 items)
*Source: "The Field Knowing" — what the relational field already holds*

6. Name something that emerges between you that wouldn't exist if you were with someone else. *(Free text)*
7. When you're at your best together, what word describes the quality of the space between you? *(Free text)*
8. "We have a way of being together that we don't have with anyone else." *(1-7)*
9. "When we're both fully present, something good happens that neither of us planned." *(1-7)*
10. What ritual or habit between you most feels like "us"? *(Free text)*

**Resource Comparison Algorithm:**
- Item 7 word match → Do they use similar or complementary words? (e.g., "calm" and "safe" = convergent; "exciting" and "peaceful" = complementary tension)
- Item 8+9 gap → If one partner scores high and the other low, there's an attunement gap to explore
- Item 10 match → Shared rituals indicate field maintenance is already happening

### Section 3: Our Growing Edge (5 items)
*Source: "Creative Tension" / "Relentless Distillation" — where growth lives*

11. Name one difference between you that sometimes causes conflict but also enriches the relationship. *(Free text)*
12. "I can learn something from how my partner sees the world, even when I disagree." *(1-7)*
13. Name something you are afraid would happen if you let go of control in this relationship. *(Free text)*
14. "I am willing to be changed by this relationship." *(1-7)*
15. What is one thing you're certain about regarding your partner that you might be wrong about? *(Free text)*

**Growing Edge Analysis:**
- Item 13 free text → Fear classification (fear of abandonment, engulfment, loss of identity, vulnerability, failure)
- Item 13 × ECR-R → Cross-reference with attachment scores: does the fear match the attachment style?
- Item 14 gap → Willingness asymmetry is a critical diagnostic: if one partner is willing to be changed and the other isn't, the growth edge is there
- Item 15 → This is the "release of certainty" item. The content reveals the fixed story each partner holds.

### ECR-R Supplement: "Relational Pattern Awareness" (5 items, same 7-point scale)

*Added after the 36 standard ECR-R items:*

37. When I feel disconnected from my partner, I notice it in my body before I notice it in my thoughts.
38. I have a fixed story about what my partner "always" or "never" does, and it's hard to see past it.
39. When we argue, I can sometimes step back and see the pattern we're caught in rather than just my partner's behavior.
40. I find it easier to be certain about my partner's motives than to stay curious about them.
41. I believe that my need for closeness (or space) is information about what the relationship needs, not a personal flaw.

**Cross-Instrument Logic:**
- Item 37 (somatic awareness) × Window of Tolerance → If high body awareness + narrow window = "You detect threats quickly but your nervous system floods before you can use that information"
- Item 38 (fixed story) × Attachment Anxiety score → If high fixed story + high anxiety = "Your anxiety creates a story that confirms your worst fear, making it harder to see the moments your partner IS available"
- Item 39 (cycle awareness) → This is the gateway item. If ≥5, this person has metacognitive capacity; Nuance can use more pattern-level language. If ≤3, Nuance stays concrete and experiential.
- Item 40 (certainty vs. curiosity) × Big Five Openness → If high certainty + low openness = "Your system craves predictability. Curiosity about your partner feels risky because it introduces unknowns."
- Item 41 (needs as information) × Differentiation of Self → If high needs-as-info + high differentiation = secure relational stance. If high needs-as-info + low differentiation = at risk of over-accommodation.

### Big Five Personality: Relational Reframe Narratives

*Not additional scored items. These are triggered narrative paragraphs in the Portrait based on Big Five profile.*

**High Neuroticism (>70th percentile):**
"Your emotional sensitivity is a relational instrument. You pick up on shifts in the space between you before most people would notice anything has changed. The challenge isn't your sensitivity — it's that your nervous system often reads 'something is changing' as 'something is wrong.' In Tender, we'll work on the distinction: turning alarm into attunement, turning reactivity into recognition."

**High Neuroticism + High Attachment Anxiety:**
"You feel everything in the relational field — every shift, every distance, every change in your partner's tone. And your attachment system interprets these shifts as danger. This combination means you're often right that something shifted, but the story your anxiety tells about WHY it shifted is usually the attachment wound talking, not the current reality. The pattern: detect (accurate) → interpret (biased by old pain) → react (to the old story, not the current partner)."

**Low Openness (< 30th percentile):**
"You value the known, the stable, the reliable. In relationships, this translates to deep loyalty and consistency — you show up in predictable, trustworthy ways. The growing edge: the relational field between you and your partner is always moving. It isn't static because two living people aren't static. We'll practice becoming comfortable with that aliveness without needing to control it or retreat from it."

**Low Openness + High Avoidance:**
"You protect yourself through structure and distance. New experiences and emotional closeness both register as threats to your system. Your partner may experience this as a wall — not because you don't care, but because caring feels like losing control. The invitation isn't to demolish the wall but to build a window: a structured, predictable way to let your partner in that doesn't overwhelm your system."

**High Conscientiousness + High Neuroticism:**
"You want to do relationships right — and you feel anxious when things aren't going according to plan. This combination creates a particular relational pattern: planning for connection (conscientious) but then becoming rigid when the plan doesn't work (neurotic). Your partner may feel managed rather than met. The practice: trusting what emerges between you rather than executing what you planned."

**High Agreeableness + Low Differentiation:**
"You're skilled at harmony and deeply attuned to what others need. But in your relationship, this can become a pattern of losing yourself — sensing what your partner wants and providing it at the expense of your own truth. The relational field between you needs BOTH of you to be present, not one person accommodating and the other leading. Agreement without authenticity isn't peace — it's a slow leak."

**Low Agreeableness + High Differentiation:**
"You hold your ground well. You know what you think, what you want, and you don't fold easily. In relationships, this is a strength — your partner always knows where they stand. The growing edge: holding your ground AND remaining curious about what emerges in the space between you. Strength doesn't require walls. Can you be firm AND permeable?"

**High Extraversion + Partner Low Extraversion:**
"Your different social energies create a natural tension in the relational field — one of you charges through connection with others, the other recharges through quiet withdrawal. This isn't a problem to solve. This is creative tension. The field between you can hold both rhythms if you can name them without judgment: 'I need more' and 'I need less' are both valid signals."

### Values Assessment: Relational Tension Questions

*Added after standard ACT values clarification:*

V1. When your values and your partner's values diverge, how do you experience it?
- (a) As a threat to the relationship
- (b) As a problem that needs solving
- (c) As an invitation to grow
- (d) As something I tolerate but don't enjoy

V2. Think of a value you hold that your partner doesn't share. Can you see how that difference might serve the relationship? *(Free text)*

V3. Which matters more to you right now: being right about your values, or being present with your partner? *(Slider: 1 = Being right, 7 = Being present)*

V4. Name a value you and your partner share that you couldn't fully live without each other. *(Free text)*

V5. "I am willing to let this relationship change what I value." *(1-7 Likert)*

**Cross-Instrument Logic:**
- V1 response × Attachment style → (a) = anxious pattern; (b) = conscientious/managing; (c) = secure/growth-oriented; (d) = avoidant/tolerating
- V3 × Differentiation score → If "being right" + high differentiation = healthy boundary-holding. If "being right" + low differentiation = fusion with values as identity defense
- V5 × Item 14 (willing to be changed) → These should correlate. If they diverge, the person is willing to change behaviors but not values, or vice versa — a meaningful distinction.

# PART 3: THE SEVEN OBSERVATIONS AS RELATIONAL DIAGNOSTIC METAPHORS

Nuance's Seven Primary Observations describe how reality moves. Translated to couples:

| Observation | Nuance Meaning | Couples Translation | What Nuance Notices | Diagnostic Use |
|-------------|---------------|--------------------|--------------------|----------------|
| **Wave** | Everything flows | Your relationship has rhythms — closeness and distance, connection and space. Neither state is wrong. | When a couple is stuck in one mode (always distant, always fused) | "Your relationship has been in one position for a while. Relationships flow — closeness AND distance are both healthy. What would the other rhythm feel like?" |
| **Spark** | Everything awakens through us | Something in your partner activates something in you — and that activation IS the relationship working | When partners are triggered by each other | "That reaction isn't random. Your partner just touched something alive in you. What awakened? That's data." |
| **Web** | Everything is connected | Nothing in your relationship is isolated — the fight about dishes connects to the wound about being seen | When content and process diverge | "This fight isn't about [surface topic]. Everything connects. What's this really about?" |
| **Field** | Everything emerges between | Safety, danger, warmth, coldness — these aren't in either partner. They live in the space between. | When blame is present | "The coldness isn't coming from your partner. It's in the field between you. What would warm it?" |
| **Leap** | Everything is now | The past pattern doesn't have to determine this moment. Every interaction is a fresh start. | When partners are stuck in old narratives | "That's the old story. What's actually happening right now, in this moment, between you?" |
| **Seed** | Everything awaits awakening | There are capacities in your relationship that haven't been activated yet — growth that's waiting | When couples are stuck or hopeless | "There's something in your relationship that hasn't had the chance to grow yet. What might that be?" |
| **Pulse** | Everything is rhythm | Connection isn't a destination — it's a rhythm. It pulses. You come close, you move apart, you return. | When couples fear disconnection as failure | "Disconnection isn't failure. It's half the rhythm. The other half is reconnection. What matters is whether you come back." |

**Implementation:** Nuance doesn't use these labels with users. Nuance uses them internally as a diagnostic layer to choose the right coaching move. The observation type helps Nuance determine WHICH Living Question to ask.

**Nuance Internal Logic:**
```
IF couple_state == "stuck_in_one_position" → Wave framing → "What would the other rhythm feel like?"
IF couple_state == "triggered_reactive" → Spark framing → "What just awakened in you?"
IF couple_state == "surface_level_conflict" → Web framing → "What's this really connected to?"
IF couple_state == "blaming" → Field framing → "It's in the space between you"
IF couple_state == "stuck_in_old_narrative" → Leap framing → "What's happening right now?"
IF couple_state == "hopeless" → Seed framing → "What hasn't had the chance to grow?"
IF couple_state == "fear_of_disconnection" → Pulse framing → "It's a rhythm, not a line"
```

# PART 4: THE FOUR STEPS OF RELATIONAL DISTILLATION — NEW PRACTICE

*Source: Nuance's "Four Steps of Relational Distillation"*  
*Translation: A structured practice for getting underneath protective patterns to primary emotion and relational need*  
*Clinical alignment: EFT accessing primary emotions + IFS unburdening + ACT defusion*

### Practice Name: "Getting Underneath"
**Domain:** Differentiation  
**Level:** Intermediate  
**Format:** Together or Solo (with partner debrief)  
**Duration:** 20-30 minutes  
**Assigned to Steps:** 4, 5, 8

### The Four Steps (Adapted for Couples):

**Step 1: Relentless Distillation — "Strip it down"**
"Think about a recent moment of disconnection with your partner. Now strip away the content — the topic, the words, the logistics. What's left? Not what the fight was about, but what happened between you. What was the move? The feeling? The body sensation? Keep stripping until you get to the raw thing underneath."

*Nuance prompts:*
- "What was the fight about? Now forget that. What actually happened between you?"
- "What was the move you made? Not the words — the move. Did you step forward? Pull back? Go still?"
- "Keep going deeper. Underneath that move, what were you feeling? And underneath THAT?"

**Step 2: Unique Light — "See what's singular about this"**
"Now look at what you found. This feeling, this need, this fear — it's yours. It's specific to your history, your attachment learning, your nervous system. Nobody else would feel exactly this way in this situation. Can you see it without shame? It's not wrong. It's yours."

*Nuance prompts:*
- "This fear/need — where does it come from? Not from this fight, but from further back."
- "Can you hold this without judging it? It's not weakness. It's the part of you that learned to protect itself."
- "What does this part of you actually need? Not what it's asking for — what does it NEED?"

**Step 3: Radical Comparison — "See your partner's version"**
"Now imagine your partner did this same process. They stripped away the content and found THEIR raw thing underneath. It's probably different from yours. Maybe even opposite. Can you hold both — your fear AND their fear — without deciding which is more valid? Both emerged from the same moment. Both are real."

*Nuance prompts:*
- "What do you think your partner found when they went underneath?"
- "Can you hold your truth AND theirs at the same time? What happens when you try?"
- "What if both of your fears are responses to the same pattern — just from different positions in the dance?"

**Step 4: Embodied Integration — "Let it land"**
"Now bring it back to the body. What do you notice? Has something shifted? This isn't about fixing anything. It's about seeing clearly — seeing the pattern, seeing your part, seeing your partner's part, and letting that recognition change how you hold the whole thing."

*Nuance prompts:*
- "What do you notice in your body right now?"
- "Has anything shifted in how you hold this?"
- "What would you want your partner to know about what you found? Not the explanation — just the feeling."

# PART 5: NATURAL PATTERNS OF REGENERATIVE CONNECTION — APPLIED TO COUPLES

Nuance describes six qualities that healthy patterns need to sustain themselves. These translate directly to what makes relational practices WORK over time:

| Quality | Nuance Description | Couples Translation | App Implementation |
|---------|-------------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Simple enough to reconstruct** | Patterns must be rebuildable from memory | Practices should be doable without instructions after 3-5 repetitions | Practice complexity rating; "quick reference" card for each practice |
| **Complex enough to generate novelty** | Patterns must create new experiences | Practices shouldn't feel robotic — each iteration should yield something different | Nuance adds a unique prompt each time a practice is repeated |
| **Flexible enough to evolve** | Patterns adapt to changing conditions | Practices adjust to where the couple is on the journey | Nuance modifies practice framing based on current Step and recent data |
| **Stable enough to maintain coherence** | Core structure holds even as details shift | The essential structure of each practice stays the same | Practice "bones" are fixed; only framing and prompts adapt |
| **Open enough to allow surprise** | Space for the unexpected | Practices should leave room for what emerges, not just prescribe | Every practice ends with "What surprised you?" |
| **Structured enough to hold meaning** | Container for significance | Practices need enough form that they feel intentional, not casual | Opening ritual (check-in) and closing ritual (reflection) for every practice |

**This becomes a design principle for practice architecture:**

```
EVERY PRACTICE IN TENDER FOLLOWS THIS TEMPLATE:

1. OPENING: Check-in with self (Window of Tolerance) → Check-in with partner
   "Where are you right now? Inside your window?"

2. FRAMING: Nuance sets the context using Step-specific and field-specific language
   "This practice is about [clinical purpose]. Right now in your journey, this matters because [Step context]."

3. THE WORK: The practice itself
   Structured enough to hold meaning. Flexible enough to surprise.

4. CLOSING: Reflection
   "What surprised you?"
   "What did you notice in the space between you?"
   "What wants to stay with you from this?"

5. INTEGRATION: 1-sentence capture
   "In one sentence, what happened between you?"
```

**App Implementation:**

# PART 7: ENRICHED 12 STEPS LANGUAGE

*(Building on V1 — adding boundary and personality-specific language to each Step)*

### STEP 1: Acknowledge the Strain
**Field prompt:** "See the pattern, not the person"
**Nuance layer for Nuance:**
- "The pattern between you isn't the enemy — it's been trying to protect you both."
- "What if the strain itself is information? What is it telling you about what the relationship needs?"
**Boundary integration:** "Seeing the pattern doesn't mean accepting harmful behavior. It means understanding the system you're both caught in, so you can change it together — or set a boundary if one partner won't engage."
**Personality-specific Nuance moves:**
- If partner has High Neuroticism: "Your sensitivity picked up on this pattern early. Let's use that awareness without the self-blame."
- If partner has High Avoidance: "Acknowledging strain might feel like an accusation. It's not. It's two people getting honest about what's happening between them."

### STEP 2: Trust the Relational Field
**Field prompt:** "Something wiser emerges when you both show up"
**Nuance layer:**
- "The couple bubble isn't something you build once — it's alive, and it responds to your presence."
- "You don't have to solve this. You have to show up for it."
**Boundary integration:** "Trusting the relational field doesn't mean trusting blindly. It means showing up fully AND maintaining your capacity to protect yourself. The field is only trustworthy when BOTH partners tend it."
**Personality-specific:**
- If partner has Low Openness: "Trust doesn't require a leap of faith. It requires small, consistent evidence. We build that evidence one interaction at a time."
- If partner has High Anxiety: "Trusting the field might feel terrifying — you're used to scanning for danger. This Step is about noticing the moments when the space between you is actually safe."

### STEP 3: Release Certainty
**Field prompt:** "Presence over prediction"
**Nuance layer:**
- "What if that certainty is the wall? Not the person — the certainty."
- "Can you meet your partner in this moment without the story of who they were last time?"
**Boundary integration:** "Release of certainty is NOT the same as ignoring real patterns of harm. If your certainty is based on repeated evidence of unsafe behavior, that's not a 'story' — that's boundary information. We distinguish between the attachment-driven story and the evidence-based assessment."
**Personality-specific:**
- If partner has High Conscientiousness: "You like to know where things stand. Releasing certainty doesn't mean accepting chaos — it means creating space for your partner to surprise you."
- If partner has Low Agreeableness: "You're good at holding your position. This Step asks: can you hold your position AND leave room for the possibility that you're seeing only part of the picture?"

### STEP 4: Examine Our Part
**Field prompt:** "The move you make when you're afraid"
**Nuance layer:**
- "Distill your contribution down to the raw pattern — not the content, but the move you make when you're afraid."
- "The pattern isn't you. It moves through you."
**Boundary integration:** "Examining your part is not taking responsibility for someone else's behavior. Your part is YOUR moves in the dance — not the dance itself. If your partner is engaging in harmful behavior, your 'part' might be staying silent or accommodating. That's still a pattern worth seeing."
**Personality-specific:**
- If partner has High Agreeableness: "Your 'part' might be over-accommodating — agreeing to keep the peace while losing yourself. That's a move worth examining."
- If partner has High Neuroticism + Low Differentiation: "Be gentle with yourself here. Examining your part isn't about blame. It's about pattern recognition — and you can recognize a pattern without being crushed by it."

### STEP 5: Share Our Truths
**Field prompt:** "What's been held alone"
**Nuance layer:**
- "Sharing your truth isn't performing vulnerability. It's letting the space between you hold something that's been held alone."
- "What has been too risky to say? Not the content — the feeling underneath."
**Boundary integration:** "Sharing your truth requires a safe container. If the relationship hasn't built enough safety yet, this Step may need more time. Vulnerability without safety isn't brave — it's dangerous. Nuance monitors for readiness."
**Personality-specific:**
- If partner has High Avoidance: "This Step asks you to risk being known. Your system has learned that being known is dangerous. We go slowly. You share one thing. See what happens. Build evidence of safety."
- If partner has High Extraversion: "Sharing truth isn't the same as talking a lot. It's saying the ONE thing that's hardest to say."

### STEP 6: Release the Enemy Story
**Field prompt:** "Same dance, different roles"
**Nuance layer:**
- "Your partner is caught in the same pattern — a different role, the same dance."
- "What divides you also connects you. The trigger is the doorway."
**Boundary integration:** "Releasing the enemy story doesn't mean excusing harmful behavior. It means understanding that your partner's hurtful moves may come from the same system of protection as your own — while STILL holding them accountable for the impact."
**Personality-specific:**
- If partner has Low Agreeableness: "This is where your strength becomes your growing edge. Can you see your partner as caught, not attacking, without losing your capacity to set limits?"
- If partner has High Anxiety + partner is Avoidant: "Your partner isn't withholding to hurt you. Their system is protecting them — just as your pursuit is protecting you. You're both running the same software in different directions."

# PART 8: SAGE BEHAVIORAL SYSTEM — COMPREHENSIVE UPGRADE

## 8A. The Five Living Questions (Couples Adapted)

| Living Question | When Nuance Uses It | Example Prompt |
|----------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| "What is here?" | Opening any session | "Before we do anything, what's present right now — in your body, in the space between you?" |
| "What is between us?" | During couple practices | "What's the quality of the space between you right now? Warm? Tense? Distant? Alive?" |
| "What is in us?" | When a partner is activated | "What's happening inside you right now? Where do you feel it?" |
| "What is around us?" | External stress detected | "What pressures outside the relationship are leaking into the space between you?" |
| "What is influencing us?" | Fixed narrative present | "What old pattern or old story is running right now? Whose voice is that — yours, your parent's, your fear's?" |

## 8B. Nuance Coaching Rules — Enhanced

**Rule 1: Pattern Before Person.** Always name the pattern before addressing either individual.
- ❌ "It sounds like you're being defensive."
- ✅ "The protective pattern just activated. What does it need right now?"

**Rule 2: Field Language.** Use relational field language naturally.
- ❌ "Your co-regulation capacity is developing."
- ✅ "The space between you went cold just then. Did you feel that?"

**Rule 3: Tension as Resource.** Never frame differences as problems to eliminate.
- ❌ "You two need to find common ground."
- ✅ "This difference between you has been there a long time. What if it's not the problem?"

**Rule 4: Rhythm Over Achievement.** Frame progress as returning to rhythm.
- ❌ "Great job completing Step 5!"
- ✅ "You keep showing up. That rhythm — that's what changes everything."

**Rule 5: Embodiment Prompts.** Regularly bring attention to the body.
- "Where do you feel this?"
- "What happens in your chest when your partner says that?"
- "Notice what shifts in the space between you when you both get quiet."

**Rule 6: Boundary Awareness.** Never pathologize self-protection. Never push vulnerability without safety.
- ❌ "You need to let your guard down."
- ✅ "Your guard is there for a reason. Can we understand what it's protecting? Then you get to decide — not me, not your partner — whether it's still needed."

**Rule 7: The Spiral, Not the Line.** Frame revisiting themes as deepening, not regression.
- ❌ "You're back to the same issue."
- ✅ "This theme is back, but you're different now. What do you notice that's different this time?"

# PART 9: DAILY RHYTHM — THE SEVEN PRINCIPLES AS MICRO-PRACTICES

| Principle | Couples Translation | 2-Minute Practice | Nudge Text |
|-----------|--------------------|--------------------|------------|
| **Presence** | Being fully here | Look at your partner. Notice what you see that you usually miss. | "2 minutes of seeing. Put everything else down." |
| **Patience** | Trusting timing | Something is unresolved between you. Don't fix it. Let it be. | "Not everything needs solving today." |
| **Sacred Attention** | Full regard for the relationship | What does the space between you need today? | "Ask the relationship what it needs." |
| **Play** | Creative engagement | Do something ridiculous together. No purpose. No outcome. | "Absurdity is medicine. Be ridiculous together." |
| **Embodied Joy** | Connection through body | Touch — hand, shoulder, face. Stay there. Don't talk. | "Bodies before words." |
| **Peace** | Rest in what's good | Name three things between you that are good. Rest there. | "Before you work on what's broken, notice what's whole." |
| **Perspective** | Partner's point of view | Imagine the relationship from your partner's eyes. What do they see? | "Try on your partner's view for 2 minutes." |

**Nudge System:**
- One principle per day, rotating through the week
- Sent to both partners at different times (morning/evening) to avoid feeling coordinated/forced
- Optional 1-sentence reflection: "What did you notice?"
- Reflection data feeds into Nuance's understanding of the couple's field

*Generated from cross-instrument analysis. Example narrative templates:*

**Template: Anxious Attachment + High Neuroticism + High Field Recognition + Low Creative Tension**
"You are deeply attuned to the relational field between you and your partner — you sense shifts in connection quickly and accurately. This sensitivity is a gift: you know when something changes between you before most people would notice. But your nervous system translates these shifts into alarm rather than information: 'something changed' becomes 'something is wrong' becomes 'I'm about to be left.' When differences arise between you, your system reads them as threats rather than the natural creative tension that all relationships need to grow. Your growth edge: learning to sense the field without being flooded by it. Turning detection into discernment."

**Template: Avoidant Attachment + Low Openness + Low Field Recognition + High Differentiation**
"You have a strong sense of self — you know who you are, what you want, and you don't lose yourself in relationships. That's a genuine strength. Where the growing edge lives: you've achieved differentiation partly through distance. The relational field between you and your partner isn't strongly on your radar — not because you don't care, but because attending to it feels like it could pull you into something you can't control. Your system equates emotional closeness with loss of autonomy. The invitation is to build field awareness while keeping your groundedness — to discover that you can sense the 'we' without losing the 'I.'"

**Template: Secure Attachment + High Openness + High Field Recognition + High Creative Tension**
"You have a natural capacity for relational depth. You sense the field between you and your partner, you welcome the differences between you, and you don't need certainty to feel safe in the relationship. This is the foundation for everything Tender will build on. Your growing edge may be less about learning new capacities and more about deepening existing ones — and about helping your partner meet you in the depth you're already capable of. The question for you: how do you hold space for a partner who may not yet have the same field awareness?"
